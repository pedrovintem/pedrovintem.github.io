Dark Light

Religious composers (holy minimalism)

John Tavener – The Lamb
I liked this song. I didn’t know John Tavener until today, and I was very impressed by this choral piece. It was peaceful, calm, and it seemed to be written to enjoy the echo of a church. This means that I didn’t feel sudden changes in the voices. Instead, they seemed to float on their own and slowly fade out.

Arvo P√§rt – Spiegel im Spiegel
Another piece that I enjoyed, and I didn’t know the composer. The piano is very simple, but I think its purpose is to allow the violin to express long sustained notes. I can imagine this being played at a church, and the sound echoing through the walls. I have the desire to understand how deeply rooted this song is in Estonian traditional music.

Henryk G√≥recki – Symphony No 3
The music seems very peaceful but mournful at the same time, especially in the middle of the first movement when the soprano starts singing. I liked that the first half of the first movement seems like it is building up for a big explosion, but it never arrives. Overall, I think this symphony could be a soundtrack for a movie or documentary. I believe Zimmer thought so too.

Minimalism

Steve Reich – Music for 18 Musicians
I find this type of music harder to enjoy. The simple sound and repetitive nature with subtle changes are not my preference. I understand the exploration of sounds, similar to Ravel’s Bolero, and can appreciate it to some extent. However, the length of the repetition is too much for me. Listening to it as a whole might induce a trance-like feeling and it made me wonder if this kind of music could also be used for religious purposes.

Terry Riley – In C
I tried to listen attentively to the entire piece, but once again, aside from the interesting exercise, I don’t find this art form truly enjoyable. What stood out to me was how the introduction of new sounds and instruments took the music on different paths. This was more noticeable when I skipped ahead a few minutes from time to time. I understand that the composer probably didn’t intend his music to be listened to this way, but for me, it provided a more interesting experience.

Philip Glass – Glassworks
I found this album much easier to appreciate than the previous two. Although there is still repetition, there is a sense of progression. It feels like the music is going somewhere. Some moments reminded me of Radiohead, which added another layer of interest. Another notable aspect of Glass’s music is the varied tempo compared to the previous pieces, creating interesting contrasts between songs.

New complexity

Brian Ferneyhough – Exordium
It’s difficult for me to write about this piece. I felt uncomfortable at times and wasn’t sure if I liked it or not. It seemed highly complex and lacked direction after my initial hearing. It felt more like an exploration of sounds rather than a cohesive composition. While I acknowledge that there is certainly thought behind this piece, I couldn’t fully grasp it.

Choral music/tonality

John Rutter – Requiem
All of Rutter’s Requiem felt accessible to me. Even though I wasn’t familiar with the artist, there was a sense of d√©j√† vu while listening to the pieces. It reminded me of G√≥recki, and I could imagine it as a movie soundtrack. The harmonies are simple, and the instrumentation is understated. However, this simplicity doesn’t diminish the emotional impact. For example, Agnus Dei felt mysterious and sombre to me.

Eric Whitacre – Cloudburst
The introduction was surprising and bewildering, with the gradual rise of all the sounds. I couldn’t determine if it was purely vocal or if other instruments were involved. I enjoyed how the voices floated at times, each having its own line, but I was even more captivated when they harmonized and created a powerful sonority.

Microtonality and spectralism

Tristan Murail – Allegories
I have limited knowledge of microtonality and spectralism, so this is a new experience for me. This particular piece left me uncertain about my feelings by the end. I attempted to identify recurring elements, but I struggled to do so. Instead, I found myself focusing on the electronic sounds and trying to differentiate them from the acoustic sounds. It was challenging for me to discern between the two. I couldn’t help but wonder if this blurring of sounds was intentional on the part of the composer.
