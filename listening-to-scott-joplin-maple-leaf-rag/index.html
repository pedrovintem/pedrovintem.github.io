Key takeaways

  • Scott Joplin was a remarkable composer who was able to combine African musical influences with classical music from Europe.
  • Syncopation adds variety and forward movement to music, making it feel faster even when the tempos aren’t.
  • The I-V progression is useful for conveying comfort and familiarity.

When researching for black composers I came across Scott Joplin, whom, I must admit, I didn’t know much of his work. Although I had heard some ragtime music before, I was far from knowing what exactly makes a ragtime song and how important it was to modern popular music.

Joplin’s History

Scott Joplin was born in northeastern Texas in 1867 or 1868, one of the first generation of African Americans born after slavery was abolished. His father used to be a slave, but his mother was born free. Both of Scott’s parents were performers, and he and his siblings grew up surrounded by music, primarily African traditional music performed by slaves.

Scott’s mother recognized his interest in music and was able to find him a free music teacher in exchange for housework. Julius Weiss, a German immigrant who, according to other students, was a follower of the European musical heritage with a special fondness for opera, was his teacher. Scott Joplin’s music owed a great deal to this. A descendant of an African slaver was not supposed to get a formal education, much less musical education. However, it was because of Weiss that he was able to understand piano technique, music theory, and, most crucially, European musical knowledge such as opera (Berlin, nd.).

The role of Joplin in Rag music

There is no doubt that Scott Joplin’s music took on the fresh feel it had because of the convergence of African and European music. He combined the syncopation of Afro-American folk music with the harmonization and marching tempos of 19th-century European romanticism to form the ultimate combination (Jasen & Tichenor, 1989). As a result, this led him to ragtime.

According to Grove Music Online, ragtime is “a style of popular music that flourished from the mid-1890s to 1918. Its main identifying trait is its ragged—i.e., syncopated—rhythm” (Berlin, 2013).

Joplin was known for his rags, particularly Maple Leaf Rag. Although it was not the first rag to be published, it was one of the most influential compositions in establishing ragtime as the first African-American music style to be widely adopted, earning him the title of King of Ragtime.

The Maple Leaf Rag and many other Joplin’s rags are considered Classic Rags. By “classic” we consider that the rag has a structure that “has three or four sections, each featuring a different 16-measure musical theme, or melody. In turn, these equal sections are divided into four lines of four measures each. Once a theme is played through, it is generally repeated immediately; in certain cases, it reappears later in the piece.” (The Library of Congress, Classic Rag).

Maple Leaf Rag is a good example of a Classic Rag since it not only follows the usual AABBACCDD pattern, is in duple meter, and has significant syncopation in the melody, but it is also very well thought out. To perform this composition successfully, you’ll need a developed pianistic talent.

Scott’s publisher, John Stark, believed his publications were as good as best classical music, and he promoted his view in elaborate ads. “Why are the Stark Music Co’s Rags called Classic? This is the reason: They are intellectual musical thoughts grounded in the emotional principle of humanity.” (The Library of Congress, Classic Rag).

My listening of Maple Leaf Rag

This composition drew my attention since Joplin defines the rhythm, speed, and atmosphere of the music from the first bar. He doesn’t build up any anticipation, and there aren’t many opportunities for both hands to rest. After playing this rag, I can image a pianist becoming really fatigued.

However, I believe that the absence of build-up, along with the melody’s powerful syncopation, results in a danceable and incredibly catchy music. At the same time, for individuals who are used to classical music and marches, the continuous simple bass progression (which is usually an I-V progression) provides a very familiar and comforting feel.

Philosophically, I believe the contrast between the syncopated melody and the structured bass reflects Joplin’s position in the artistic culture of the period. On the one hand, having been raised in an African musical background, he came from a culture where syncopated percussion beats are very prominent and where rhythm is vital to making people dance and interact with music. On the other hand, he was given formal classical training that allowed him to learn how the best European classical composers composed their works. The Maple Leaf Rag and others demonstrate that he learned from both cultures and passed them on to the composition.

You can clearly hear the syncopation and the I-V progression in Maple Leaf Rag’s first bars in the A section, where the bass line plays a steady rhythm, while the melody plays a syncopated rhythm. The syncopation is prominent in the song throughout.

Another key point to note is that, like previous classical composers, Joplin knew how to generate tension and release it. My past hearing of folk African music led me to believe that it was heavily reliant on repetition rather than dramatic moments. In bars 7-9, you can sense the tension building up.

In the B section, Joplin inserts chromatism to give the music a fresh perspective and, in my opinion, to instill the composition with some of his musical understanding.

The modulation from Ab Major to Db Major at the beginning of the C section and back to Ab at the beginning of the D section, especially the last one, is done quite smoothly. Although the modulation to the subdominant is a common ragtime modulation, I feel Joplin handled it in a fluid manner to once again demonstrate his abilities.

Another aspect of this music that I found intriguing is the triumphal finale, which has a counter motion between the voices and a large chord that eventually allows the pianist to rest after demonstrating his abilities.

Upon hearing Scott Joplin and reading about him, I concluded that he always fought for recognition. Despite the fact that Rags were not considered high-quality music, he had a formal musical education. I believe that confrontation is what caused his pieces to be considered so well-written, timeless, and a template for other styles that grew from it, including jazz.

After having studied both Joseph Bologne and Scott Joplin I find their similarities and differences very insightful. Both came from one musical background and were later exposed to another. But it did not impact them in the same way. The novelty and merge of cultures I was expecting, was more evident in Joplin’s music. My point is not that one is better or more relevant than the other. The two composers were inspirations and essential to composers who came after them. But when you think of music as an evolving landscape of different artistic viewpoints, I believe Joplin played a significant role in showing composers that you could mix them all and create beautiful pieces of music.
