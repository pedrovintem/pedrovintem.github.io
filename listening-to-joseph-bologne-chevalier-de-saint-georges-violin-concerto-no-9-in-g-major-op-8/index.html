Key takeaways

  • There are black composers who don’t get the attention they deserve, and Joseph Bologne is one of them
  • I couldn’t fiind an African inspired sound in his works, maybe because of his European education
  • I find descending notes pleasing, once again

One thought occurred to me while I was searching for music to listen to. What is the reason for the scarcity of well-known African classical composers? I couldn’t think of one, and a quick Google search reveals that the first dozen names that come up are all white men. I recognize that my lack of knowledge stems primarily from my western culture, so I set out to find black composers, which I am glad I did.

One of the articles I read was from classicfm.com entitled “9 Black composers who changed the course of classical music history”. The first featured composer is Joseph Bologne, Chevalier de Saint-Georges.

Joseph Bologne was born to a wealthy plantation owner and his Senegalese slave mistress in Basse-Terre, Guadaloupe, in 1745. He received the best education his father could afford during the family’s move to France and grew into an exceptionally gifted student. He had professional training, as evidenced by his extensive work list, which includes a number of comedic operas, symphonies and concertos, as well as chamber works (Banat, 2001).

Despite his pejorative nickname of The Black Mozart (also known as The Other Mozart), I find myself wondering if it shouldn’t be the other way around. Joseph Bologne is regarded as one of the greatest composers of the 18th century, according to Marlon Daniel. He was known to frequent salons as a guest of the French nobility, including those of queen Marie Antoinette, who was an accomplished singer herself. He influenced Franz Joseph Haydn, François-Joseph Gossec, Carl Stamitz, and, most notably, Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart, making him a leader rather than a follower of trends in the musical evolution of his century (Daniel, 2021).

His work was innovative since his earlier compositions, according to a New York Times article. “The six-string quartets of his Opus 1 were among the first in that genre to be written in France. His three sonatas for keyboard and violin (Op. 1a) feature those instruments as equals, breaking away from the Baroque tradition of basso continuo, which was still very much in vogue” (Balter, 2020).

Despite how innovative Bologne’s work may have sounded after listening to his violin concertos, my mind constantly tells me that he sounds a lot like some of his contemporaries. This, I believe, is a case of knowledge bias.

His Violin Concerto No. 9 in G Major, Op. 8 is one of my favourite works. I was struck by the three movements’ energy and rhythm, as well as the motifs he created. I couldn’t find a free score for this piece, but they can easily compete with other well-known motifs, in my humble and naive opinion. Why hasn’t his music been used in an advertisement commercial?

The second movement, Largo, is one of my favourites. Despite the fact that it is a slower movement, I consider it to be a continuation of the first movement’s accentuated rhythm. The syncopation provides a solid sound of descending notes in the first minute, which I find pleasing. In fact, I find this movement to be quite contemporary. While listening to him, I noticed parallels with Nicolas Britell’s, in my opinion, amazing Succession Tv Show soundtrack, particularly the Adagio in C Minor I show below.

In the end, I was thrilled about this discovery. I’m not qualified to say whether Joseph Bologne was a better composer than Mozart, but after hearing some of his works, I believe he deserves at least more credit.
