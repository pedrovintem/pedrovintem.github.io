Exercise 4: Identifying the Main Points

  • Music vs. Musicology:
    • Music is a practical activity with a long history.
    • Musicology is the study and reflection on music, with a relatively shorter history.

  • Definition of Musicology
    • Broadly defined as the thinking about and study of music
    • Stands outside the creative process to provide perspective on:
      • The creative process itself
      • The end product (musical work)
      • Social and cultural contexts

  • Historical Development of Musicology:
    • Early figures like Forkel and F√©tis proposed early forms of musicology.
    • Guido Adler (1885) formalised musicology, separating it into historical and systematic dimensions.
    • The historical dimension involves organizing music history into epochs and regions.
    • The systematic dimension focuses on internal properties like harmony and tonality.

  • Expansion of Scope
    • Traditional focus on Western classical music
    • Now includes ethnomusicology, jazz, and popular music studies
    • Reflects a more inclusive “whole musical field” approach

  • Historical Narrative of Musicology:
    • Traces origins back to ancient Greece (Plato and Aristotle) and through medieval, Renaissance, and Enlightenment periods.
    • The Enlightenment and 19th-century Romanticism influenced musicology with a focus on historicism and aesthetics.
    • Modernism in the 20th century brought systematic study and musicology as a discipline.

  • Contemporary Musicology:
    • Joseph Kerman’s work in the 1980s marked a significant shift.
    • Kerman critiqued formalism and positivism in musicology, advocating for a more humane criticism.
    • The field has become more critical, interdisciplinary, and focused on interpretation rather than mere facts.

  • Current State of Musicology:
    • Embraces a broad range of music and critical models.
    • Reflects ongoing changes and expansions in the field.
    • Provides a stimulating and motivational context for current and future research.
