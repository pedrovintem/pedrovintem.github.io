CMP Assignment 3: Colour Composition

In this composition, I explored the timbres of bass clarinet, xylophone, electric guitar, piano, and cello, aiming for a balance of warmth, depth, brightness, and sharpness. The clarinet and cello provided a solid, resonant foundation, while the xylophone and electric guitar added a crisp, bright contrast. The piano served as a blend between these timbres. One moment where the contrast of timbres stood out was starting from bar 48, where the bass clarinet and cello played in octaves, answered by the xylophone, piano, and electric guitar. This created a unique conversation between the five instruments.

The piece is largely monophonic and, maybe inspired by the exercise of Klangfarbenmelodie, I was led to explore how the different timbres could produce varied sonorities through using instruments to complement each other. Additionally, the rhythmic part in this composition was influenced by Fred Onovwerosuoke’s “Twenty-Four Studies in African Rhythms,” which motivated me to incorporate more rhythm into the piece.

Dynamically, I wanted to establish a sense of rising in the introduction through a long crescendo from piano to sforzando. The silences I used during this section created a dramatic contrast, adding an element of unpredictability to the fast-paced introduction‚Äîlike to the anticipation of a rollercoaster ride. Octave doublings between the xylophone, piano, and guitar also enriched the sound, particularly at the end of each section.

Compared to my previous two-note exercise, the addition of Bb in this composition allowed for more melodic freedom, giving the piece a D minor feel. While the pitch limitation initially was a challenge, it encouraged me to focus on rhythm, timbre and dynamics rather than relying on harmonic and melodic variety.

An interesting aspect of this composition was the influence of hip-hop and rap. This influence became clear as I composed, with the rhythmic focus and minimalism of these genres aligning well with the pitch restrictions.

Overall, I appreciate certain aspects more than others, such as the silences and the use of tremolandos at the end.
