It doesn’t hurt trying to do something new.

When I was researching the tambourine, I came across Rodion Shchedrin’s Carmen Suite for string orchestra and percussion (1967).

A little backstory. When Shchedrin’s wife, Maya Plisetskaya, asked Shostakovich to write a new Carmen ballet, he “gently but firmly refused.” “I’m afraid of Bizet.” After that, she asked her husband, who agreed. Shostakovich’s response demonstrates Shchedrin’s bravery. After its debut performance, the Carmen Suite was deemed “disrespectful” to Bizet’s opera and was banned by the Soviet leadership. (Plisetskaya, 2001)

I feel that percussion, such as tubular bells, cymbals and castanets, are used throughout Shchedrin’s score to emphasize rhythms. Although he pays homage to the original material, he frequently pushes the music in unexpected ways, heightening the atmosphere and drama.

On the other side, I find the presence of percussion to be rather comic at times, such as when the timpani glissando is used or when the strings provide distinctive effects.

Overall, I think the experiment of using percussion to replace other instruments was a great success, and the end result was quite entertaining. If it’s superior to the original? I don’t believe it is necessary to respond.

It reminded me of Johnny Cash’s Hurt version. It was a risky move for producer Rick Rubin to persuade Johnny Cash to record a version of Nine Inch Nail’s Hurt. Cash refused to sing the song, which was widely regarded as one of NIN’s best. It wasn’t until he read the lyrics that Cash realized he could make his own version of the song. Finally, the singer and producer managed to create a masterpiece from a masterpiece. It wasn’t better or worse than the original; rather, it was unique. Instead of being a song about drugs and self-destruction, they transformed it into an acoustic song about the epilogue of an eventful life.

Like NIN/Cash’s Hurt, Bizet/Shchedrin’s Carmen can exist in the music world indefinitely as two different but interconnected works that don’t have to be judged against each other.

References

Plisetskaya, M., 2001. I, maya plisetskaya. [Place of publication not identified]: Yale Univ Press.
