Following several listenings and notes on the score, I selected some passages that illustrate the piece’s remarkable points.

  • There’s a declamatory opening when the voices enter for the first time, stating the phrase “The Lord says to my lord”.
  • The long melisma on the word “sede” appears first with the sopranos, is then copied by the alto and further in the piece is sang by the bass.
  • Examples of florid vocalizations are usually found in melismas, such as the one below.
  • Voices have a strong rhythmic presence, but in the exclamation of dix it, they have the strongest rhythm.

  • Especially when the voices are silent, the violins have a melody of rising and falling arpeggios.
  • In some parts, the musical textures are relatively consistent, involving the uniform use of quavers and semiquavers.


  • A big part of this movement has a regular bass and a constant rhythm that makes it feel predictable. But there are times of surprise, such as those presented below, where crochets and minims (and some rests) substitute the usual quavers and semiquavers.
