What makes a happy melody?

I was listening to music on Spotify on shuffle when Tim Maia’s Não Quero Dinheiro came on, and I had a thought: why do I feel happy every time I hear this song, particularly the chorus? I’ll try to figure it out by examining its melody.

Tim Maia was a Brazilian musician who is regarded as one of the country’s most influential musicians. Maia pioneered sambalanço with Jorge Ben, a genre that combines samba, soul, funk, and rock and roll.

In 1971, he recorded Não Quero Dinheiro, a samba/soul song in which he declares, “I don’t want any money, I just want love,” in response to rising consumerism. It’s a love song that’s likely influenced by the peace and love movement of the 1960s. The song went on to become a great success. Note that Brazil was under a dictatorship at the time, and this style of music was used as an escape from oppression.

This song catches my attention because of its general cheerfulness and happiness just by listening to it. This is, in my opinion, to do with repetition, syncopation, and tension.

The song begins with a syncopated melody that is repeated twice before the variation. This is the song’s verse, and while the tone is lively, it does not reach the climax of the chorus.

The chorus is the most joyful element of the song, but Tim Maia’s creation of a hymn with only three notes is even more astounding. And I believe that repetition is the key. In the first part of the chorus, the A is repeated 32 times.

If you listen to this melody alone, especially on the piano, you’ll hear almost a childlike repetition. As if a child was trying to prove she was better than others. It seems like you might be able to sing “My toy is better than yours” in this melody.

This chorus is also characterized by tension. The song repeats itself six times, creating tension, with the hope of resolution which comes in half of the seventh bar. The repetition-tension-resolution game continues throughout the entire melody.

I was delighted to discover this Ted Talk about music repetition by Elizabeth Margulis, Professor and Director of Princeton University’s Music Cognition Lab.

In a nutshell, repetition makes you feel that music is more enjoyable, more interesting and more human. Music critics, according to Margulis, are often embarrassed by repetition, believing it to be childish or regressive; but repetition, far from being embarrassing, helps us to think of music as a way of seeing the world.

Not to be forgotten is Beethoven’s repeated four-note theme in his Symphony No. 5 or Ravel’s Bolero, which is composed entirely of variations on a single motif.

Finally, in my opinion, what makes this melody feel happy and cheery is the same childish repetition that reminds you of a period when life was simple and happy, and all you cared about was playing and being happy. And those joyful memories are made up of repeated memories.
